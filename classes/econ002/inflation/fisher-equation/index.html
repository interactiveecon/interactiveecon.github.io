Fisher Equation & Inflation: Who Wins and Who Loses?

Explore how nominal interest, expected inflation, and unexpected inflation affect real interest rates and redistribute wealth between borrowers and lenders.

How this works

This app connects two ideas: (1) the Fisher equation (how inflation affects real interest rates) and (2) redistribution (who wins/loses when inflation is different than expected). You control the nominal interest rate and inflation rates, and the app shows what happens to a borrower/lender (a loan) and a saver (a deposit).

Step 1: Set the interest rate and inflation

  • Nominal interest rate \(i\): the posted interest rate in dollars. (In this app we restrict \(i \ge 0\).)
  • Expected inflation \(\pi^{e}\): what people think inflation will be when they sign the contract.
  • Actual inflation \(\pi\): what inflation turns out to be. If \(\pi \ne \pi^{e}\), there is an inflation surprise.

Step 2: Read the Fisher equation boxes

  • Expected real interest rate: \[ r^{e} \approx i - \pi^{e}. \] This is the real rate people expect when they agree to the nominal contract.
  • Realized real interest rate: \[ r \approx i - \pi. \] This is the real rate that actually occurs given the inflation that happens.
  • Inflation surprise: \[ \pi - \pi^{e}. \] Positive = inflation higher than expected. Negative = inflation lower than expected (possibly deflation).

Step 3: The loan panel (borrower vs lender)

Think of a one-year loan with principal \(P\). The nominal repayment is: \[ \text{Nominal repayment} = P(1+i). \] Inflation changes the purchasing power of that nominal repayment. The app converts the repayment into “Year-1 dollars” using: \[ \text{Real repayment} \approx \frac{P(1+i)}{1+\pi}. \]

  • If \(\pi > \pi^{e}\) (unexpectedly high inflation), the real value of repayment is lower than expected. Borrowers win and lenders lose.
  • If \(\pi < \pi^{e}\) (unexpectedly low inflation or deflation), the real value of repayment is higher than expected. Lenders win and borrowers lose.
  • If \(\pi = \pi^{e}\), there is no surprise, so there is no unexpected redistribution (beyond what both sides anticipated).

Step 4: The savings panel (saver vs borrower)

A savings deposit is the mirror image of a loan: the saver is essentially the lender. The app shows the saver’s nominal balance \(P(1+i)\) and its real purchasing power \(P(1+i)/(1+\pi)\).

  • Unexpectedly high inflation (\(\pi > \pi^{e}\)) reduces real purchasing power → saver loses.
  • Unexpectedly low inflation/deflation (\(\pi < \pi^{e}\)) increases real purchasing power → saver gains.

How to use the app (recommended workflow)

  1. Start with \(\pi = \pi^{e}\). Notice \(r \approx r^{e}\) and the app says “as expected.”
  2. Increase \(\pi\) while holding \(\pi^{e}\) fixed. Watch the inflation surprise turn positive and see the borrower win.
  3. Decrease \(\pi\) below \(\pi^{e}\) (and into deflation if you want). Watch the surprise turn negative and see the lender/saver win.
  4. Now adjust \(i\). Notice how higher \(i\) raises both nominal repayments and (holding inflation fixed) the real rate.
  5. Use Random scenario to practice quickly, then answer the Check yourself questions.

Note: We use the common approximation \(r \approx i-\pi\). For small rates, this is very close to the exact Fisher relationship.
