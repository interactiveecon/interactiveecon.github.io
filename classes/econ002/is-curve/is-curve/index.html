Keynesian Cross, IS Curve, and Fiscal Policy

Instructions
  1. Start a round. Click New Scenario. The app will tell you which policy variable changed: r, G, or T, and whether it moved up or down. (The sliders reset to baseline each round.)
  2. Apply the shock with the sliders. Move the relevant slider(s) to reflect the scenario shock. As you move sliders, both graphs update:
    • Blue = baseline (original) lines and equilibrium.
    • Orange = current lines and new equilibrium.
  3. Read the Keynesian Cross (top graph). The top graph plots Planned Expenditure (PE) against Output (Y). The 45° line is where PE = Y. The economy is in equilibrium at the intersection:
    • Y₀ = baseline equilibrium output (blue dot).
    • Y₁ = new equilibrium output (orange dot, after your slider change).
    The dashed vertical lines show the equilibrium output on the x-axis.
  4. Read the IS curve (bottom graph). The IS curve shows combinations of (Y, r) consistent with goods-market equilibrium. The dots show the linked equilibrium points:
    • (Y₀, r₀) is the baseline point (blue).
    • (Y₁, r₁) is the new point (orange).
    The dashed lines project each dot to the x-axis (output) and y-axis (interest rate).
  5. Build the mechanism chain. Use the pill cards to explain why output changes. Drag the cards into the slots, or click a card to select it then click a slot to place it (keyboard-friendly).
  6. Check your reasoning. Click Check Mechanism to see if your chain matches the scenario. Use Clear Chain to try again, or Reset to return everything to baseline.

Tip: The key idea is that shocks first change planned expenditure. When spending and production don't match, firms experience an unplanned inventory change, which leads them to adjust production, changing output (Y).
