Edgeworth Box Explorer — Exchange Efficiency

Jerry and Elaine split a fixed bundle of 10 bowls of cereal and 8 pancakes. Drag the allocation point inside the Edgeworth box to see how the two consumers' indifference curves shift, where gains from trade still exist, and when an allocation is Pareto-efficient.

How to use this lab

The Edgeworth box visualises every possible way to divide the total endowment of 10 cereal and 8 pancakes between Jerry and Elaine. Jerry's origin is the top-right corner; Elaine's origin is the bottom-left corner.

  1. The blue curves are Jerry's indifference curves; the red curves are Elaine's. Each curve traces allocations the consumer ranks equally.
  2. The blue dot is the current allocation. Read the consumers' holdings on each axis, or look at the status strip below the box.
  3. When the allocation is inefficient, a light-green lens region appears — those are allocations strictly preferred by both consumers, i.e. mutually beneficial trades from the current point.
  4. An allocation is Pareto-efficient when Jerry's marginal rate of substitution (MRS) equals Elaine's MRS, so no trade can make either consumer better off without hurting the other.
  5. Answer the questions in order. Each new problem randomises both consumers' preferences (the Cobb-Douglas weights α and β shown under the box) and the starting allocation. The contract curve is hidden until you finish question 2.
  6. Press New Problem at the bottom of the page to generate a fresh randomized setup at any time.
